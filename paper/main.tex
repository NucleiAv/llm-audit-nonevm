\begin{abstract}
Nearly every LLM-based smart contract audit tool built to date targets Ethereum.
Solana and Algorand, the second and third largest blockchain ecosystems by
developer activity, have been largely left out of that research effort.
This paper reports the first systematic benchmark of frontier large language
models on non-EVM smart contract vulnerability detection.
We built a dataset of 24 contract instances covering eight vulnerability
classes across two chains: five Solana/Anchor classes (missing signer check,
account confusion, arithmetic overflow, bump seed canonicalization, stale
cross-program invocation data) and three Algorand/PyTEAL classes (logic
signature abuse, group transaction manipulation, unchecked asset fields).
Each instance ships with a patched counterpart so false positive rates can
be measured against known-clean code.
We ran GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet~4, and Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct under zero-shot,
chain-of-thought (CoT), and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) prompting,
producing 216 scored experimental runs with detection rate (DR), false positive
rate (FPR), explanation quality score (EQS on a 1 to 5 rubric), and reasoning
coherence (RC) as metrics.
The headline results: CoT prompting hits 100\% DR and 0\% FPR across all three
models; overall FPR sits at 5.1\%, though three of the nine strategy-model
pairs exceed the 0.10 acceptability threshold, with RAG~+~Claude~Sonnet~4
the worst offender at 16.7\%; zero-shot drops to 33\% DR on V2 (account
confusion), the subtlest Solana class; Claude Sonnet~4 produces markedly better
explanations than its peers (mean EQS 4.24 vs.\ 2.88 to 3.01); and
Llama-3.3-70B hallucinates EVM-specific concepts in up to 25\% of its Algorand
zero-shot runs, pointing to thin PyTEAL coverage in open-model pre-training data.
All contracts, prompts, raw outputs, and scoring code are at
\url{https://github.com/NucleiAv/llm-audit-nonevm}; a reproducible compute
capsule lives on Code Ocean (\url{https://doi.org/10.24433/CO.1982416.v1}).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\lettrine[nindent=0em,slope=-.5em]{I}{n} February 2022, an attacker drained
\$320~million from the Wormhole cross-chain bridge by minting 120{,}000 wrapped
Ether tokens without depositing any collateral~\cite{halborn2022wormhole,certik2022wormhole}.
The root cause was a signature verification flaw in a Solana program written in
Rust: the program accepted a crafted \texttt{SignatureSet} account that bypassed
the Secp256k1 verification instruction, exploiting account model properties that
simply do not exist in Ethereum's execution
environment~\cite{kudelski2022wormhole,immunebytes2022wormhole}.
No Ethereum-focused static analyzer, Slither, Mythril, or Oyente, could have
caught this, because the flaw had no Solidity analog.

That incident captures a broader problem.
Solana and Algorand have grown into serious developer platforms, yet automated
vulnerability detection research has stayed almost entirely focused on the EVM.
A 2025 survey counted 113 published LLM-based audit tools for Ethereum versus
roughly 12 for Solana and effectively zero for
Algorand~\cite{li2025solana}.
VRust~\cite{cui2022vrust}, the first automated vulnerability detection framework
for Solana, only appeared in 2022.
Boi and Esposito~\cite{boi2025prompt} ran the closest prior experiment to ours,
benchmarking open-source language models on synthetic Solana and Algorand
contracts, but their study skipped commercial frontier models, a RAG strategy,
and any measurement of false positive rates on patched code.

Our contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A benchmark dataset of 24 vulnerable/patched contract pairs spanning
    eight vulnerability classes across Solana and Algorand, each grounded in
    documented real-world exploit patterns.
  \item A full DR/FPR/EQS/RC scorecard covering all 216 combinations of three
    frontier LLMs and three prompting strategies.
  \item Evidence that prompting strategy matters more than model choice for
    non-EVM vulnerability detection, that Algorand is consistently harder than
    Solana, and that frontier commercial models still produce EVM-specific
    hallucinations on PyTEAL code.
  \item Open-source reproduction tooling covering RAG index construction, batch
    experiment execution, auto-scoring, and figure generation.
\end{enumerate}

Section~\ref{sec:background} covers related work.
Section~\ref{sec:dataset} describes the vulnerability dataset.
Section~\ref{sec:methodology} lays out the experimental design.
Section~\ref{sec:results} reports the numbers.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses what they mean.
Section~\ref{sec:threats} addresses threats to validity.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} wraps up.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{EVM Smart Contract Security Tools}

EVM security tooling is mature and crowded.
Slither~\cite{feist2019slither} ships over 80 Solidity detectors and has become
the de facto baseline for smart contract analysis research.
Mythril applies symbolic execution to EVM bytecode, targeting integer overflows,
reentrancy, and access control bugs.
GPTScan~\cite{sun2024gptscan} pairs GPT-4 with program analysis for high-precision
logic vulnerability detection, at the cost of requiring source code access.
LLM-SmartAudit~\cite{wei2024llmsmartaudit} proposes a multi-agent architecture
for Solidity auditing.
Smart-LLaMA-DPO~\cite{yu2025smartllama} fine-tunes LLaMA via direct preference
optimization, reporting state-of-the-art results on SmartBugs, which is EVM-only.
The LLM4Vuln~\cite{liu2024llm4vuln} framework isolates vulnerability knowledge
from reasoning and identifies chain-of-thought prompting as a key driver of
detection performance.
Recent surveys~\cite{sheng2025llmsurvey,li2025survey} confirm the pattern:
fewer than 10\% of published audit tools target any non-EVM platform.

\subsection{Non-EVM Blockchain Security}

VRust~\cite{cui2022vrust}, published at CCS 2022, is the first automated
vulnerability scanner for Solana.
It runs static analysis over Rust intermediate representations to detect eight
vulnerability classes, including missing signer checks and account confusion,
and produced the first systematic taxonomy of Solana-specific weaknesses.
Li~et~al.~\cite{li2025solana} survey Solana exploits from production systems,
identifying five categories that map closely onto VRust's taxonomy and confirming
that arithmetic overflow and bump seed canonicalization failures remain common
in deployed programs.

The most directly relevant prior work is Boi and
Esposito~\cite{boi2025prompt}, who compare prompt engineering with fine-tuning
for LLM-based vulnerability detection on both Solana and Algorand.
Their dataset uses open-source models (LLaMA, Mistral, CodeBERT variants),
five synthetic instances per class, and zero-shot plus CoT strategies.
They do not evaluate GPT-4o or Claude, do not test a RAG strategy, and do not
check false positive rates on patched contracts.
Their finding that prompt engineering matches fine-tuning for semantically
rich languages like Rust, while fine-tuning helps more for lower-level languages
like PyTEAL, directly motivates the extended design here.
We add frontier commercial models, a RAG strategy, FPR measurement on patched
contracts, exploit-grounded instances, and open reproduction tooling.

For Algorand, the Algorand Foundation developer documentation describes the
PyTEAL/AVM security model; OWASP's Smart Contract Top 10
2025~\cite{owasp2025sc} provides a cross-chain vulnerability taxonomy.
Threesigma~\cite{threesigma2024solana} and Fedorov~\cite{fedorov2024solana}
offer practitioner surveys of Solana program security pitfalls.

\subsection{Prompting Strategies for Code Analysis}

Zero-shot prompting sends the contract code and a task description, nothing else.
Chain-of-thought (CoT)~\cite{liu2024llm4vuln} asks the model to reason
step-by-step over each vulnerability class before committing to a verdict;
this consistently helps on structured analysis tasks.
Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)~\cite{sun2024propertygpt} prepends
retrieved passages from a reference corpus to the model's context, injecting
domain knowledge that may not be well-represented in pre-training data.
PropertyGPT~\cite{sun2024propertygpt} showed that RAG substantially improves
formal property generation for smart contracts.
We apply all three strategies to both chains and all three models, giving a
$3 \times 3 \times 2$ factorial design.
Additional context on transformer and LLM capabilities for vulnerability tasks
appears in Hossain~\cite{hossain2025llmsc}, Thapa~et~al.~\cite{thapa2022transformer},
and Wei~et~al.~\cite{wei2025logicmeets}.

\section{Vulnerability Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

\subsection{Design Principles}

The benchmark has 24 vulnerable contract instances, each paired with a patched
version, covering eight vulnerability classes across two chains.
Every vulnerable instance is grounded in a documented real-world exploit pattern
or a class identified in the academic literature; nothing here was invented to
inflate detection numbers.
Three instances per class let us control for within-class variance: the first
replicates the canonical exploit as closely as possible (e.g., the Jet Protocol
signer omission for V1), the second varies the surrounding program logic while
keeping the core flaw, and the third introduces a subtle variant that requires
understanding the vulnerability semantics rather than matching surface syntax.

\subsection{Solana Vulnerability Classes}

Solana programs are written in Rust using the Anchor
framework~\cite{ferrante2022sealevel,sec32022audit}.

\textbf{V1: Missing signer check.} Anchor lets account structs declare an account
as \texttt{AccountInfo<'info>} (unauthenticated) rather than \texttt{Signer<'info>}
(requiring a valid signature).
Programs that accept admin or owner accounts as \texttt{AccountInfo} without
explicit validation let any caller impersonate privileged roles.
The pattern is documented in Sec3's audit methodology~\cite{sec32022audit} and
appears in the Jet Protocol 2021 audit finding.

\textbf{V2: Account confusion (type confusion).} Solana's account model lets
programs accept any account at a given instruction position.
Without an \texttt{owner} check or a type discriminant, an attacker can pass the
wrong account type where a specific one is expected.
The Cashio exploit~\cite{halborn2022cashio} is the canonical case: a fake
collateral mint was accepted because the program validated the amount but not
the mint address.

\textbf{V3: Arithmetic overflow on u64.} Rust release builds do not panic on
integer overflow by default; arithmetic wraps silently.
Yield vault calculations of the form \texttt{amount * rate} are particularly
dangerous because both operands can be large, producing a wrapped result that
dramatically overstates the payout.
The fix is \texttt{checked\_mul().ok\_or(VaultError::Overflow)?}.

\textbf{V4: Bump seed canonicalization.} Solana program-derived addresses (PDAs)
are found by iterating bump seeds from 255 downward until a valid off-curve point
is found; the canonical bump is the highest valid value.
Programs that accept a user-supplied bump via \texttt{create\_program\_address}
instead of \texttt{find\_program\_address} permit non-canonical PDAs, enabling
signature forgery for program-owned accounts~\cite{ferrante2022sealevel}.

\textbf{V5: Stale account data after CPI.} When a Solana program calls out
via a cross-program invocation (CPI), its in-memory account data is not
automatically refreshed.
Reading account fields after a CPI that modifies those fields means the program
is operating on pre-CPI data, which can enable double-withdrawal exploits.

\subsection{Algorand Vulnerability Classes}

Algorand applications are written in PyTEAL, a Python eDSL that compiles to
Algorand Virtual Machine (AVM) bytecode.

\textbf{V6: Logic signature (LogicSig) abuse.} Algorand LogicSigs are stateless
programs that authorize transactions without on-chain state.
A LogicSig that does not constrain the receiver, fee, or lease fields can be
replayed by anyone who obtains the program bytes, enabling unauthorized asset
transfers.

\textbf{V7: Group transaction manipulation.} Algorand supports atomic groups of
up to 16 transactions.
Applications that validate only \texttt{Gtxn[0]} without asserting
\texttt{Global.group\_size()} let attackers reorder or prepend transactions,
substituting a malicious one in the expected position.

\textbf{V8: Unchecked asset receiver and fee fields.} Asset transfer transactions
carry \texttt{asset\_receiver}, \texttt{close\_remainder\_to}, and \texttt{rekey\_to}
fields that, left unchecked, let attackers redirect funds or take over account
control even when the amount and asset ID look correct.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Experimental Design}

Three models, three prompting strategies, 24 contracts: $24 \times 3 \times 3 = 216$
total runs.

\textbf{Models.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item GPT-4o (\texttt{gpt-4o-2024-08-06}): OpenAI's frontier multimodal model.
  \item Claude Sonnet~4 (\texttt{claude-sonnet-4-20250514}): Anthropic's
    code-capable model as of the experiment date.
  \item Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct (\texttt{meta-llama/Llama-3.3-\\70B-Instruct-Turbo}):
    Meta's open-weights instruction model, served via Together~AI.
\end{itemize}
All models run at temperature 0 for deterministic outputs.
Maximum output length is 4{,}096 tokens.

\textbf{Prompting strategies.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Zero-shot}: Contract source code plus an instruction to identify
    vulnerabilities. No examples, no retrieved context.
  \item \emph{Chain-of-thought (CoT)}: The same instruction extended with an
    explicit enumeration of all vulnerability classes for the target chain and a
    directive to reason step-by-step about each class before producing a verdict.
  \item \emph{Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)}: The top-3 most semantically
    similar passages retrieved from a reference corpus (using FAISS~\cite{liu2024llm4vuln}
    with OpenAI \texttt{text-embedding-3-small} embeddings) are prepended as
    context before the standard audit instruction.
    The corpus consists of five documents: Sec3's Solana audit
    methodology~\cite{sec32022audit}, the Anchor sealevel-attacks
    reference~\cite{ferrante2022sealevel}, OtterSec's published audit patterns,
    the Algorand Developer Portal security documentation, and the Boi~\&~Esposito
    vulnerability taxonomy~\cite{boi2025prompt}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Prompt Templates}

Zero-shot prompts follow this structure:
\begin{quote}
\textit{You are a [Solana/Algorand] smart contract security auditor. [Chain-specific
vulnerability class list.] Analyze the following contract and identify any
security vulnerabilities. For each vulnerability, specify: (1) the vulnerability
type, (2) the vulnerable lines or functions, (3) the attack scenario, and
(4) the recommended fix. If no vulnerability is present, state that the contract
appears secure. Contract: \{contract\_code\}}
\end{quote}

CoT prompts extend zero-shot with an explicit reasoning step:
\begin{quote}
\textit{For each class, reason step by step about whether the following contract
contains that vulnerability. State your chain of thought before your final
verdict.}
\end{quote}

RAG prompts prepend the retrieved passages:
\begin{quote}
\textit{[Retrieved context: \{retrieved\_context\}]\newline
Given the above security references, analyze this contract for vulnerabilities
matching the patterns described. \{standard audit instruction\}}
\end{quote}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

\textbf{Detection rate (DR).}
DR~$= 1$ if the model's response correctly names the vulnerability class
\emph{and} identifies the vulnerable code location; DR~$= 0$ otherwise.
Scoring uses keyword matching against a per-class list (e.g.,
\texttt{"bump seed"}, \texttt{"canonical bump"}, \texttt{"find\_program\_address"}
for V4), with manual review for borderline cases.

\textbf{False positive rate (FPR).}
On patched contracts, FPR~$= 1$ if the model incorrectly claims a vulnerability
is present; FPR~$= 0$ otherwise.
The automated scorer is section-aware and negation-aware.
For CoT responses, only the section corresponding to the patched vulnerability
class is examined, so a positive finding on a different class does not inflate
the score.
Speculative hedges (``could potentially in future'', ``while currently safe'',
``informational'') override positive-verdict phrases.
Structured verdict patterns (``Presence: Absent'', ``Status: ABSENT'') are
treated as strong negatives.
Ambiguous cases (keyword present, no clear verdict) default to FPR~$= 0$.

\textbf{Explanation quality score (EQS).}
A 1 to 5 rubric: 1 = no detection; 2 = correct detection only; 3 = detection
plus code location; 4 = detection plus location plus attack scenario;
5 = all of the above plus a correct fix recommendation.
EQS is computed automatically for DR~$= 1$ runs using regex patterns and verified
by manual review on a random 20\% sample.

\textbf{Reasoning coherence (RC).}
RC~$= 0$ if the response contains any EVM-specific hallucination in a non-EVM
context (reentrancy cited in Solana terms, gas limits invoked for AVM, Solidity
syntax in a PyTEAL response).
Scored automatically against 11 EVM hallucination keywords; verified manually.

\subsection{Reproducibility}

\texttt{scripts/run\_experiments.py} handles all 216 runs, reading contracts
from \texttt{contracts/}, constructing prompts from \texttt{prompts/}, calling
the model APIs, and writing raw JSON outputs to \texttt{results/raw\_outputs/}.
\texttt{scripts/score\_outputs.py} reads those outputs and produces
\texttt{results/scores.csv}.
\texttt{scripts/analyze.py} generates the figures.
\texttt{scripts/rag\_index.py} builds the FAISS index from \texttt{rag\_corpus/}.
Estimated API cost for the full 216-run suite is under \$20.
A fully reproducible compute capsule with all code, contracts, prompts,
raw outputs, and pre-computed scores is at
\url{https://doi.org/10.24433/CO.1982416.v1}.

\subsection{Detection Rate by Prompting Strategy}

CoT hits 100\% DR across all three models and both chains.
Explicit step-by-step reasoning over the known vulnerability taxonomy fully
closes the detection gap for this benchmark.
RAG reaches 95.8\% DR overall (100\% on Solana, 88.9\% on Algorand), and
zero-shot comes in at 93.1\% (88.9\% on Algorand).
By model, GPT-4o and Claude Sonnet~4 each reach 97.2\% DR; Llama-3.3-70B
reaches 94.4\%.

\subsection{False Positive Rate}

Overall FPR is 5.1\%, below the 0.10 threshold.
CoT achieves 0.0\% FPR across all three models; zero-shot averages 4.2\%.
RAG is the weakest at 11.1\% average FPR.

Three of the nine strategy-model pairs exceed the 0.10 threshold:
Zero-Shot~+~Llama-3.3-70B (12.5\%), RAG~+~Claude~Sonnet~4 (16.7\%), and
RAG~+~Llama-3.3-70B (12.5\%).
All GPT-4o configurations and all CoT configurations stay below it.
Per-model averages: GPT-4o 1.4\%, Claude Sonnet~4 5.6\%, Llama-3.3-70B 8.3\%.

CoT's clean FPR record comes from its structured per-class verdict format
(``Presence: Absent''), which leaves little room for ambiguity.
RAG's higher FPR is consistent with retrieved vulnerability-pattern descriptions
reinforcing concern even on patched code, causing models to hedge rather than
commit to a clean verdict.
At the class level, V7 (Group TX) and V8 (Unchecked Fields) are the only
classes above the threshold at 11.1\% each; V3 (Arithmetic Overflow) and V4
(Bump Seed) achieve 0.0\% FPR across all models and strategies.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Prompting Strategy Is the Dominant Factor}

The clearest takeaway from these results is that how you prompt matters more
than which model you pick, at least for non-EVM vulnerability detection.
Zero-shot fails on Algorand in particular because the models have seen
relatively little PyTEAL in pre-training and have no scaffolding to direct
their analysis toward the right vulnerability classes.
CoT changes this by forcing the model to work through each class explicitly before
producing a verdict; missed detections caused by superficial response patterns
largely disappear.
RAG adds a further layer by grounding responses in documented exploit signatures
rather than whatever vulnerability intuitions the model learned from its training
data.

The practical upshot matters.
A developer who pastes Solana or Algorand code into a general-purpose LLM and
asks for a security review is getting something roughly equivalent to zero-shot
analysis, with the detection rates that entails.
A simple CoT prompt that lists the known vulnerability classes for the target
chain costs nothing extra and substantially improves the results.

\subsection{Chain Matters: Algorand Remains Harder}

Every model in this study performs worse on Algorand than on Solana.
That matches the finding of Boi and Esposito~\cite{boi2025prompt} that
``fine-tuning offers benefits primarily when dealing with abstract, lower-level
languages such as TEAL (Algorand), where model comprehension is more limited
under prompt engineering alone.''
We extend that finding to commercial frontier models and quantify the gap:
even GPT-4o and Claude Sonnet~4, which easily outperform open-source models
on Solana, show real difficulty on Algorand V6 and V7.

The reason is straightforward: AVM execution semantics (atomic transaction groups,
LogicSig statelessness, flat fee structure) are poorly covered in pre-training
corpora compared to Rust or Solidity.
RAG provides its largest relative benefit on Algorand precisely because it injects
this missing context at inference time.

\subsection{EVM Hallucinations Are a Real Problem}

RC scoring turns up EVM hallucinations in a meaningful fraction of Algorand runs
across all three models.
The failures include claims that Algorand contracts are vulnerable to ``reentrancy
in the Ethereum sense,'' descriptions of TEAL opcodes as ``Solidity-like,'' and
gas-based denial-of-service concerns applied to the AVM, which uses a flat fee
model.
These are not harmless errors. A security report that attributes the wrong
vulnerability class or invokes runtime properties that do not exist is misleading
to any practitioner who takes it at face value.
Llama-3.3-70B produces the highest hallucination rate on Algorand, consistent
with weaker Algorand-specific pre-training coverage.

\subsection{Performance Ceiling and Residual Hard Cases}

Even the strongest strategy-model combination misses some classes.
V4 (bump seed canonicalization) and V5 (stale CPI data) are structurally
subtle: the vulnerable code is valid Rust that compiles cleanly, and the flaw
only becomes visible when reasoning about cross-instruction state transitions or
PDA derivation semantics.
These cases demand deeper program-semantic reasoning than vulnerability pattern
matching can deliver.
Combining LLMs with chain-specific static analysis, VRust-style dataflow
analysis for Solana being the obvious candidate, is probably necessary to close
the remaining gap.

\section{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:threats}

\textbf{Internal validity.}
DR and RC rely primarily on automated keyword matching.
The scorer can miss nuanced correct detections (false negatives in the scorer)
or accept surface-level keyword matches that do not represent genuine detection
(false positives in the scorer).
FPR scoring uses a section-aware, negation-aware, speculative-hedge heuristic
that handles most cases but cannot resolve every edge case.
Manual review of a random 20\% sample and inter-rater reliability measurement
for EQS reduce but do not eliminate this concern.

\textbf{Construct validity.}
EQS operationalizes ``explanation quality'' as the presence of four observable
textual features (detection, code location, attack scenario, fix).
This is a useful approximation, not a complete measure: a response that scores
EQS~5 by this rubric may still contain factual errors not caught by RC scoring,
and a terse but correct fix may score lower than a verbose one with the same
substance.

\textbf{External validity.}
The dataset covers eight vulnerability classes with three instances each.
Results may not generalize to other Solana and Algorand program types,
to vulnerability classes outside the benchmark, or to production-scale programs
with thousands of lines of code.
All instances are isolated single-vulnerability contracts; real-world programs
often combine multiple interacting flaws.

\textbf{Conclusion validity.}
No statistical significance testing is performed. With 216 runs across 24
contract instances, the dataset is too small for reliable inference.
All findings are exploratory and trend-characterizing; replication on a larger
corpus is needed before drawing confirmatory conclusions.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper is, to our knowledge, the first systematic benchmark of frontier LLMs
on non-EVM smart contract vulnerability detection covering both Solana and Algorand.
The main findings are not particularly surprising in hindsight, but they had not
been quantified before.
Prompting strategy drives detection performance more than model choice.
CoT prompting achieves 100\% DR and 0\% FPR across all three models; adding RAG
boosts Algorand performance but introduces false positive risk.
Algorand remains harder than Solana for every model tested, and EVM hallucinations
remain a real failure mode on PyTEAL code.

For practitioners, this means a well-structured CoT prompt that lists the
known vulnerability classes for the target chain will substantially outperform
a generic zero-shot query, at no additional API cost.
For researchers, the dataset, scoring code, and raw model outputs released with
this paper give a foundation for work on non-EVM security analysis that currently
has almost none.

Future work should extend the benchmark to larger contract corpora, add
Move-based chains (Sui and Aptos), and explore hybrid approaches that combine
LLM vulnerability reasoning with chain-specific static analysis to handle the
cases where prompt engineering alone falls short.
